Password Security Best Practices

  • Length over complexity — 16+ characters is more secure than a short complex password
  • Unique passwords — never reuse passwords across accounts
  • Password manager — use Bitwarden, 1Password, or Dashlane to manage credentials securely
  • Enable 2FA everywhere — adds a critical second layer of protection
  • Passphrases work — "correct-horse-battery-staple" is strong and memorable
  • Check for breaches — visit haveibeenpwned.com to see if your email was compromised
  • Never share passwords — not via email, SMS, or chat — ever
  • Change after breaches — update passwords immediately if notified of a breach

Common Phishing Red Flags

  • Generic greeting — "Dear Customer" instead of your real name
  • Urgency & pressure — "Act now or your account will be closed in 24 hours"
  • Sender spoofing — email domain doesn't match the real company
  • Suspicious links — hover over links to see the real URL before clicking
  • Credential requests — legitimate companies never ask for passwords via email
  • Grammar & spelling errors — professional companies proofread their communications
  • Unexpected attachments — never open files you didn't expect to receive
  • Too-good-to-be-true offers — prize wins, gift cards, and "security alerts" out of nowhere

Common Scam Patterns

  • Too good to be true — unexpected lottery wins, inheritances, or "free" gifts
  • Gift card payment requests — no legitimate business asks you to pay with gift cards
  • Wire transfer requests — nearly impossible to recover once sent
  • Advance fee fraud — pay a small fee upfront to claim a much larger reward
  • Impersonation — fake IRS, bank, Microsoft, or tech support calls/messages
  • Fake investment returns — "guaranteed profits" and "risk-free" schemes
  • Romance scams — online relationships that eventually ask for money
  • Sensitive data requests — SSN, bank account, or credit card numbers

Phishing URL Warning Signs

  • Suspicious TLDs — .tk, .ml, .cf, .gq, .pw domains are frequently abused in phishing campaigns
  • Brand impersonation — paypa1-secure.com or amazon-update.tk are not legitimate sites
  • URL shorteners — bit.ly, tinyurl hide the real destination; expand before clicking
  • IP-based domains — legitimate services use named domains, not raw IPs like 192.168.1.1
  • @ symbol in URL — anything before @ is ignored by browsers; attackers use it to fake the host
  • Excessive hyphens — secure-login-update-verify.com is a classic phishing domain pattern
  • Suspicious path terms — /login, /verify, /update on unknown domains often harvest credentials
  • Heavy URL encoding — many %-sequences often conceal malicious destinations

Device Security Checklist

Harden your devices against the most common threats

Software & Updates
  • Enable automatic OS updates — most attacks exploit known, patched vulnerabilities
  • Keep browsers, plugins, and apps updated; remove unused extensions
  • Install reputable antivirus software and schedule weekly scans
  • Enable the built-in firewall (Windows Defender Firewall / macOS Firewall)
  • Uninstall software you no longer use — every app is a potential attack surface
Accounts & Access
  • Use a password manager (Bitwarden, 1Password) — unique passwords for every account
  • Enable multi-factor authentication on all important accounts
  • Use a standard (non-admin) user account for day-to-day tasks
  • Lock your screen whenever you step away (Win+L / Ctrl+Cmd+Q)
  • Set auto-lock to 5 minutes or less when idle
Disk & Data
  • Enable full-disk encryption (BitLocker on Windows, FileVault on macOS)
  • Follow the 3-2-1 backup rule: 3 copies, 2 media types, 1 offsite or cloud
  • Regularly test that backups can actually be restored
  • Securely wipe storage before selling or discarding old hardware
  • Encrypt USB drives that leave your home or office
Mobile Devices
  • Use a strong PIN or biometric lock — not a 4-digit PIN or swipe pattern
  • Only install apps from official stores; check permissions before granting
  • Keep iOS/Android updated — use auto-update for apps too
  • Enable remote wipe so you can erase a stolen or lost device
  • Avoid charging from untrusted USB ports (juice jacking) — carry your own charger
Privacy & Browsing
  • Use a privacy-respecting browser (Firefox, Brave) with uBlock Origin installed
  • Disable location, microphone, and camera permissions for sites that don't need them
  • Use a reputable VPN on public networks to encrypt your traffic
  • Regularly clear cookies and site data; use private/incognito mode for sensitive browsing
  • Check haveibeenpwned.com monthly to see if your email was in a breach
Incident Preparedness
  • Know your ISP and device manufacturer's support contacts in advance
  • Keep a printed note of your password manager's emergency access / recovery kit
  • Know how to isolate an infected machine from the network (disable Wi-Fi / unplug)
  • Store a clean OS boot USB in case of ransomware or corrupt system files
  • Document your accounts and services so you can recover access in an emergency

Wi-Fi Security Guide

Secure your home and workplace networks

Router Hardening
  • Change the default admin username and password on your router immediately
  • Update router firmware — manufacturers regularly patch security vulnerabilities
  • Disable remote management unless you specifically need it
  • Use WPA3 encryption if your router supports it; WPA2-AES is the minimum
  • Disable WPS (Wi-Fi Protected Setup) — it has known vulnerabilities
Network Passwords
  • Use a strong, unique Wi-Fi password — at least 16 characters with mixed characters
  • Don't share your Wi-Fi password publicly or post it on social media
  • Create a separate guest network for visitors and IoT devices
  • Regularly rotate your Wi-Fi password, especially after a security incident
  • Never use your name, address, or birth year in your network name (SSID)
Public Wi-Fi Safety
  • Assume all public Wi-Fi (cafes, airports, hotels) is untrusted and monitored
  • Use a reputable VPN whenever connecting to public networks
  • Never access banking, email, or sensitive accounts on public Wi-Fi without a VPN
  • Watch for evil twin attacks — fake hotspots named identically to legitimate ones
  • Disable auto-connect to open networks in your device's Wi-Fi settings
IoT & Smart Devices
  • Place smart TVs, cameras, and IoT devices on a separate guest VLAN
  • Change default passwords on every smart device — most ship with "admin/admin"
  • Disable features you don't use: Universal Plug and Play (UPnP) is a common risk
  • Check manufacturer websites for firmware updates for all smart home devices
  • Regularly audit devices connected to your network — remove anything unrecognised
Monitoring & Detection
  • Log in to your router's admin panel monthly to review connected devices
  • Set up router alerts for new device connections if your router supports it
  • Enable DNS-over-HTTPS (DoH) on your router or devices to prevent DNS snooping
  • Consider using Pi-hole or AdGuard Home for network-wide ad and tracker blocking
  • Review your ISP's router security features — many offer basic threat monitoring
VPN Guidance
  • Choose a VPN with a verified no-logs policy and independent audit (Mullvad, ProtonVPN)
  • Avoid free VPNs — your traffic data is often the product being sold
  • Use WireGuard or OpenVPN protocol for the best security-to-speed balance
  • Enable the VPN kill switch to block traffic if the VPN connection drops
  • A VPN protects your connection, not your accounts — still use strong passwords and 2FA

CyberGuard is an educational guidance tool. It does not provide hacking instructions, assist with unauthorised access, or replace professional incident response. For active security incidents, contact a qualified cybersecurity professional or your organisation's security team.

Privacy

Content you paste for analysis (passwords, emails, URLs, messages) is processed in memory and never stored. Only anonymous tool usage counts are logged to help improve the product. No personal data is collected or shared.

How It Works

Security scanners use heuristic pattern matching running entirely on the server — your content does not reach third-party scan APIs. The AI assistant uses the Groq or OpenAI API to generate responses.

Technical Stack

  • Backend: Python 3.10+, Flask 3.x
  • AI Provider: Groq (llama-3.3-70b-versatile) or OpenAI (gpt-4o-mini)
  • Database: SQLite (anonymous usage counts only)
  • Analysis: Heuristic-only, no external scan APIs
